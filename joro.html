About Work

Joro
UI Design and Information Architecture

Project

I was asked to help Joro with the creation of their iPhone app by designing their information and user interface architecture.

Problem

Joro had some rough sketches and mockups of what they wanted their app to look like, but needed me to adjust the visual hierarchy and produce high fidelity mockups for their engineering team.

Role

I assumed the role of a freelance product designer for this project, meeting with Joro over video conference and discussing the project via email.

Final user interface architecture for the carbon offset flow

Solution

Joro's mission is to help reduce carbon emissions by making it easy for users to purchase carbon offsets. I felt it was important to make the carbon offset user flow sleek, simple, and informative for the user.

The final user interface relies heavliy on Apple's Human Interface Guidelines. Sticking to the HIG relativly tightly gave the app a very fast and native feel and made it easier for the Joro engineers to quickly implement.

Prior User Research

Before joining this project, Joro had conducted preliminary user research to get an idea of what potential users already knew about carbon emissions, and to test some low fidelity user flows.

Looking at this information, it was clear that the UI should be clear about education and price. The users showed interest in knowing how they were helping the envirnoment through Joro and as always, exactly how much they were paying and for what.

The original logo.

Early Explorations

To begin the design process for this branding project, I started by getting a feel for their platform and sketching out some ideas for potential icons. During this stage of the design process I try to sketch any idea that comes to mind to not narrow down the scope of possibilities.

A selection of initial logo sketches.

High Fidelity Iterations

After reviewing these sketches with Slapdash, I took their feedback into consideration while moving on to higher fidelity work to give some life to the sketches.

A selection of higher fidelity mockups.

Since this was a relativly simple logo, I took the project to the high fidelity stage quickly as I was still able to rapidly iterate. These high fidelity mockups allowed me to work out details and have a frame of reference for further discussions with Slapdash.

Finishing Touches and Type

After presenting my higher fidelity mockups to Slapdash, there were some designs that stood out to them that I further worked on to make sure we were happy with every detail. At this point I started testing out different typefaces and looking at how they interacted with the logo. With hundreds of iterations in the books, I arrived at the final logo and wordmark that Slapdash was proud to launch version 1.0 with.
